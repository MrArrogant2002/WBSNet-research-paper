\begin{abstract}
Encoder--decoder medical image segmentation networks still rely on plain
skip connections that copy low-level encoder activations into a high-level
decoder, carrying a persistent semantic gap and discarding the boundary
cues that strided downsampling has already blurred. We present the
\textbf{Wavelet Boundary Skip Network (WBSNet)}, a backbone-agnostic
encoder--decoder architecture in which every skip connection is replaced
by a Wavelet Boundary Skip (WBS) module. A WBS module decomposes the
encoder feature into a low-frequency approximation sub-band and three
directional high-frequency sub-bands via a two-dimensional discrete
wavelet transform, recalibrates the approximation with a Low-Frequency
Semantic Attention (LFSA) block, and enhances the high-frequency stream
with a depthwise-separable, boundary-supervised High-Frequency Boundary
Attention (HFBA) block before reconstructing the refined skip through the
inverse transform. Four WBS modules deliver deep Sobel-derived boundary
supervision at every scale while adding only $\approx\!1.1$M parameters
on top of a ResNet-34 U-Net. On Kvasir-SEG, CVC-ClinicDB, CVC-ColonDB
(cross-dataset), and ISIC~2018, WBSNet consistently improves Dice, IoU,
and $\mathrm{HD}_{95}$ over strong CNN, Transformer, and Mamba baselines.
An ablation study isolates the wavelet decomposition as the dominant
factor for boundary accuracy---not merely ``more attention on the skip
features.'' Code and configurations are released for reproducibility.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\IEEEPARstart{S}{emantic} segmentation of polyps, skin lesions, and other
structures in clinical images is a foundational building block for
computer-aided diagnosis, margin estimation, disease tracking, and surgical
planning. In colonoscopy screening, accurate polyp segmentation supports
adenoma detection and characterisation, influencing follow-up intervals and
resection decisions. In dermoscopy, precise lesion delineation governs
excision margins and malignancy scoring. The clinical utility of an
automated segmentor is therefore tightly coupled to \emph{how faithfully it
reconstructs the boundary}, not merely how often it covers the lesion
interior.

The U-Net family~\cite{ronneberger2015unet} has dominated medical
segmentation for most of a decade because its skip connections recover
spatial detail lost to encoder downsampling. Subsequent work has explored
deeper encoders (ResNet~\cite{he2016resnet}, EfficientNet), attention
gates~\cite{oktay2018attnunet}, denser
skips~\cite{zhou2018unetpp,alom2022r2upp}, Transformer
encoders~\cite{chen2021transunet,cao2021swinunet}, and state-space
backbones~\cite{ruan2024vmunet,ma2024umamba}. Yet the skip connection itself
has changed only modestly. In the canonical U-Net a skip is a tensor-wise
\emph{identity copy}: the activation leaving the encoder stage is passed
through unchanged and concatenated with the decoder. Two failure modes
follow directly from this design.

\paragraph{Semantic gap} The encoder's shallow stages encode edges,
textures and orientation; the decoder operates on progressively more
abstract representations. Copying a low-level activation through a skip
forces the decoder to reconcile two representations that are not merely at
different resolutions but at different levels of abstraction. Prior
analyses of this phenomenon~\cite{peng2023unetv2,wang2024udtransnet} show
that a large fraction of the decoder's capacity is spent suppressing the
low-level content of the skip rather than using it.

\paragraph{Boundary blur} Each strided encoder stage halves the spatial
resolution; high-frequency content that encodes organ or lesion boundaries
is attenuated by the low-pass filtering implicit in strided convolutions
and max-pools~\cite{zhang2019makingshiftinvariant}. The skip connection
passes whatever survives, but makes no effort to \emph{emphasise} the
boundary. For polyps and dermoscopic lesions the boundary is
clinically the most important structure --- it determines extent of disease
and margin of resection. A network that averages over a blurred boundary
costs real clinical value.

A growing literature tries to repair skip connections with Transformer
attention~\cite{wang2024udtransnet,chen2021transunet}, Mamba state-space
blocks~\cite{wu2025skvmpp,ruan2024vmunet}, routed mixtures of
experts~\cite{tamosc2025}, or dense cascades~\cite{alom2022r2upp}. Other
work adds frequency awareness~\cite{li2024freqfusion,sffnet2024,waveformer2023}
or boundary priors~\cite{wanet2025,bmanet2025,bunet2023,bgglnet2025,meganet2024}
but places these beside the skip --- as a separate branch, a post-hoc
refinement head, or inside the encoder. The two ingredients are rarely
combined, and when they are~\cite{wanet2025,bcfunet2024} they live in
different modules that interact only through the decoder.

We argue that a principled skip connection should do three things: (i)
explicitly separate the ``structure'' channel from the ``boundary''
channel, (ii) attend to each according to its role, and (iii) supervise the
boundary stream with the ground truth that is already available. The
two-dimensional discrete wavelet transform (DWT) is the natural tool for
(i): it provides an exact, invertible, parameter-free decomposition into
one low-frequency sub-band (LL) and three directional high-frequency
sub-bands (LH, HL, HH). This lets us apply different attention to different
physical quantities rather than treating the skip as one undifferentiated
tensor. We propose \textbf{WBSNet} (Fig.~\ref{fig:arch}), a ResNet-34 U-Net
in which every skip connection is a \emph{Wavelet Boundary Skip} (WBS)
module.

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Unified wavelet-boundary skip.} We design a single module that
      folds DWT, channel attention on the approximation (LFSA), and
      boundary-supervised spatial attention on the merged high-frequency
      stream (HFBA) with exact IDWT reconstruction, yielding a same-shape
      drop-in replacement for any U-Net-style skip.
\item \textbf{Deep multi-scale boundary supervision.} A $1{\times}1$ head
      inside every HFBA produces boundary logits that are supervised by
      Sobel-derived edge maps at \emph{each} of four scales, rather than by
      a single auxiliary head on top of the decoder.
\item \textbf{Lightweight, backbone-agnostic design.} The depthwise-separable
      HFBA fuses the three high-frequency sub-bands into one spatial
      attention stream, keeping total WBS overhead at $\approx\!1.1$M
      parameters and preserving the inference cost of a vanilla ResNet-34
      U-Net within the margin of a single encoder stage.
\item \textbf{Thorough empirical and ablative evaluation.} On Kvasir-SEG,
      CVC-ClinicDB, the CVC-ColonDB cross-dataset protocol, and ISIC 2018
      we report Dice, IoU, precision, recall, accuracy, specificity, and
      $\mathrm{HD}_{95}$. A seven-variant ablation (A1--A7) isolates each
      design choice --- in particular A6 (attention on raw skip features
      without the wavelet) shows that the wavelet decomposition itself is
      necessary, not merely an opportunity for extra attention.
\item \textbf{Analysis.} We present a gradient-flow analysis that ties the
      WBS module's deep boundary supervision to a low-variance gradient
      reaching the encoder, visualise the per-sub-band attention weights,
      and characterise training dynamics across three seeds.
\item \textbf{Reproducibility.} We release config-driven training,
      evaluation, aggregation, and significance-testing scripts together
      with the exact YAML files used for every table and figure, plus a
      reproducibility checklist in Appendix~\ref{app:checklist}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Skip Connection Design}
\label{sec:related:skip}

U-Net++~\cite{zhou2018unetpp}'s nested dense skips and Attention
U-Net~\cite{oktay2018attnunet}'s gated skips were the first to argue that
the skip connection is a design surface, not an identity. U-Net
v2~\cite{peng2023unetv2} uses a Hadamard-product scheme to infuse
cross-level semantic and detail information; UDTransNet~\cite{wang2024udtransnet}
replaces the skip with a Dual Attention Transformer; SK-VM++~\cite{wu2025skvmpp}
inserts Mamba VSS blocks inside skip connections; TA-MoSC~\cite{tamosc2025}
routes skips through a mixture-of-experts; R2U++~\cite{alom2022r2upp} makes
skips recurrent and dense; FCBFormer~\cite{fcbformer2022} adds a
Transformer branch alongside the encoder. A recent
analysis~\cite{rethinkskips2024} shows that selectively \emph{removing} the
uppermost skip can even reduce domain-specific noise under distribution
shift. None of these approaches operate in the frequency domain, and none
explicitly supervise a boundary signal inside the skip. WBSNet sits in this
design space but differs structurally: it decomposes the skip into
LL/\{LH,HL,HH\} \emph{before} attending and reconstructs via an exact
inverse transform.

\subsection{Wavelet and Frequency-Domain Segmentation}
\label{sec:related:wavelet}

FreqFusion~\cite{li2024freqfusion} shows that frequency-aware fusion fixes
intra-category inconsistency and boundary displacement for dense
prediction. WRANet~\cite{wang2023wranet} uses the DWT to \emph{replace}
downsampling and then discards the high-frequency sub-bands to denoise ---
which is the opposite of what medical boundaries need.
MFR-UNet~\cite{mfrunet2025}, SFFNet~\cite{sffnet2024},
WaveFormer~\cite{waveformer2023}, and EW-ViT~\cite{ewvit2025} put wavelets
in the encoder or inside a Transformer's self-attention, not in the skip.
WFE~\cite{wfe2023} injects multi-scale wavelet features into the backbone.
FFTNet-style methods~\cite{rao2021fnet} operate globally with the Fourier
transform but lack directional detail sub-bands. Our WBS module places the
wavelet \emph{inside the skip}, preserves high-frequency content under
boundary supervision rather than discarding it, and does not require a
separate encoder branch.

\subsection{Boundary-Aware Networks}
\label{sec:related:boundary}

MEGANet~\cite{meganet2024} inserts Laplacian edge-guided attention at every
encoder--decoder stage, but uses a single-frequency operator.
BMANet~\cite{bmanet2025}, BRNet~\cite{brnet2024}, and
BUNet~\cite{bunet2023} derive boundary maps or uncertainty masks and feed
them to the decoder. WA-NET~\cite{wanet2025} combines an Enhanced Wavelet
Transform module with a separate Boundary Refinement Module;
EASNet~\cite{easnet2025} couples DCT and DWT with a criss-cross boundary
attention; BCF-UNet~\cite{bcfunet2024} fuses multi-frequency encoding with
a boundary-aware pyramid module; SkinMamba~\cite{skinmamba2024} binds a
Frequency Boundary Guided Module to a Mamba backbone.
BoundaryLoss~\cite{kervadec2019boundaryloss} proposes a distance-map
objective orthogonal to our Sobel-target BCE formulation. All of these
split the ``wavelet'' and ``boundary'' functions across separate modules.
WBSNet unifies them in a single skip-path module and supervises the
boundary signal at four scales from one Sobel-derived target. A summary of
positioning is in Table~\ref{tab:positioning}.

\subsection{Transformer and State-Space Backbones}
\label{sec:related:backbones}

TransUNet~\cite{chen2021transunet}, Swin-UNet~\cite{cao2021swinunet}, and
the nnFormer family~\cite{zhou2023nnformer} replace or augment the encoder
with Transformer blocks. VM-UNet~\cite{ruan2024vmunet} and
U-Mamba~\cite{ma2024umamba} do the same with state-space blocks. These
changes target long-range dependence in the encoder; the skip connections
remain identity copies or simple concatenations. WBSNet is
\emph{orthogonal} to this axis: its WBS module is a skip-level module and
composes with any of these encoders. We evaluate with ResNet-34 for a
controlled comparison with the established U-Net family, and outline
expected behaviour under stronger backbones in
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} and Appendix~\ref{app:backbones}.

\subsection{Lightweight and Mobile Segmentation}
\label{sec:related:lightweight}

PMFSNet~\cite{pmfsnet2024}, MobileUNet, and nnU-Net's~\cite{isensee2021nnu} low-resolution
configurations target edge deployment with sub-million-parameter networks,
typically at a measurable quality cost. WBSNet is not strictly a
mobile-class model but its WBS module adds only $\approx\!1.1$M parameters
and $\sim\!6$ GFLOPs to a ResNet-34 U-Net (Table~\ref{tab:flops}); we view
this regime as the sweet spot between quality and deployability for
clinical workstations.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Overall Architecture}
\label{sec:method:arch}

WBSNet follows the U-Net topology with a ResNet-34 encoder and a four-stage
decoder (Fig.~\ref{fig:arch}). Let $\mathbf{x}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{3\times
H\times W}$ be the input RGB image ($H\!=\!W\!=\!352$ in all experiments).
The encoder produces features at five resolutions: $\mathbf{e}_0$
(\emph{stem+relu}, $H/2\!\times\!W/2$, 64 channels), $\mathbf{e}_1$
(layer1, $H/4$, 64), $\mathbf{e}_2$ (layer2, $H/8$, 128), $\mathbf{e}_3$
(layer3, $H/16$, 256), and $\mathbf{e}_4$ (layer4, $H/32$, 512). The
feature $\mathbf{e}_4$ serves only as the decoder input (bottleneck). The
four other encoder stages are each refined by a dedicated WBS module,
$\tilde{\mathbf{s}}_k = \mathrm{WBS}_k(\mathbf{e}_k)$ for $k=0,1,2,3$,
and the refined skips are concatenated with progressively up-sampled
decoder features. Each decoder stage is a bilinear $\times 2$ up-sampling
followed by a concatenation with the matching $\tilde{\mathbf{s}}_k$ and
two $3{\times}3$ BN-ReLU convolutions. A final $1{\times}1$ segmentation
head produces
$\mathrm{logits}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{1\times H\times W}$ after one more
$\times 2$ up-sampling.

\subsection{The Wavelet Boundary Skip (WBS) Module}
\label{subsec:wbs}

Figure~\ref{fig:wbs} summarises the module. Given an encoder feature
$\mathbf{F}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{C\times H\times W}$ at some skip level, a WBS
module produces a refined same-shape skip $\tilde{\mathbf{F}}$ and a
boundary-logit map $\mathbf{M}_{\text{bnd}}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{1\times
H/2\times W/2}$.

\begin{figure}[!t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth]{figures/wbs_module.png}
  \caption{\textbf{WBS module.} The encoder feature $\mathbf{F}$ is
  decomposed by a 2D DWT into $\{\mathbf{LL},\mathbf{LH},\mathbf{HL},
  \mathbf{HH}\}$. LFSA recalibrates $\mathbf{LL}$ channel-wise; HFBA fuses
  the three high-frequency sub-bands, predicts a boundary-logit map, and
  gates the result with its sigmoid. The inverse DWT reconstructs a
  same-shape refined skip. The boundary logits are supervised by Sobel
  edges.}
  \label{fig:wbs}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{2D Haar DWT}
\label{subsec:wbs:dwt}

We use a single-level 2D separable Haar DWT implemented with one stride-2
$2{\times}2$ grouped convolution whose weights are the Kronecker product of
the Haar low-pass $\boldsymbol{\ell}\!=\![1,1]/\sqrt{2}$ and high-pass
$\boldsymbol{h}\!=\![-1,1]/\sqrt{2}$ vectors. Per channel this yields four
sub-bands of shape $C\!\times\!H/2\!\times\!W/2$:
\begin{equation}
\{\mathbf{LL},\mathbf{LH},\mathbf{HL},\mathbf{HH}\}=\mathrm{DWT}_{\text{Haar}}(\mathbf{F}),
\end{equation}
with no learnable parameters. In matrix form, letting $\otimes$ denote the
Kronecker product, the 2D filter bank is
\begin{equation}
\{\boldsymbol{\ell}\otimes\boldsymbol{\ell},\;\boldsymbol{\ell}\otimes\boldsymbol{h},\;\boldsymbol{h}\otimes\boldsymbol{\ell},\;\boldsymbol{h}\otimes\boldsymbol{h}\},
\end{equation}
and the reconstruction filter bank is the transposed version. Daubechies-2
(\emph{db2}) is also supported (ablation A7) and is implemented through the
same code path using \texttt{pywavelets} filter coefficients.

\subsubsection{Low-Frequency Semantic Attention (LFSA)}
\label{subsec:wbs:lfsa}

The approximation sub-band $\mathbf{LL}$ retains structural content. We
recalibrate it with a Squeeze-and-Excitation style
block~\cite{hu2018senet}:
\begin{align}
\mathbf{z} &= \mathrm{GAP}(\mathbf{LL})\in\mathbb{R}^{C\times 1\times 1},\\
\mathbf{w} &= \sigma\bigl(W_{\uparrow}\,\mathrm{ReLU}(W_{\downarrow}\mathbf{z})\bigr),\\
\widetilde{\mathbf{LL}} &= \mathbf{w}\odot\mathbf{LL},
\end{align}
where $W_{\downarrow}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{C/r\times C}$,
$W_{\uparrow}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{C\times C/r}$, $r\!=\!16$, and $\sigma$ is
the sigmoid. LFSA's parameter count is $2C^{2}/r$, which is negligible
compared to the encoder and decoder.

\subsubsection{High-Frequency Boundary Attention (HFBA)}
\label{subsec:wbs:hfba}

The three detail sub-bands are concatenated along the channel axis,
$\mathbf{H}=[\mathbf{LH};\mathbf{HL};\mathbf{HH}]\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{3C\times
H/2\times W/2}$. A depthwise-separable block reduces them to $C$ channels:
\begin{align}
\mathbf{H}' &= \mathrm{ReLU}\bigl(\mathrm{BN}\bigl(\mathrm{DW}_{3\times3}(\mathbf{H})\bigr)\bigr),\\
\mathbf{H}_{\text{red}} &= \mathrm{ReLU}\bigl(\mathrm{BN}\bigl(\mathrm{PW}_{1\times1}(\mathbf{H}')\bigr)\bigr)\in\mathbb{R}^{C\times H/2\times W/2},
\end{align}
followed by a $1{\times}1$ convolution that predicts a boundary logit map
and gates the merged high-frequency stream with its sigmoid:
\begin{align}
\mathbf{M}_{\text{bnd}} &= W_{\text{b}}\,\mathbf{H}_{\text{red}}\in\mathbb{R}^{1\times H/2\times W/2},\\
\widetilde{\mathbf{H}} &= \sigma(\mathbf{M}_{\text{bnd}})\odot\mathbf{H}_{\text{red}}.
\end{align}
We reuse the single attended stream for all three IDWT high-frequency
slots, $\widetilde{\mathbf{LH}}\!=\!\widetilde{\mathbf{HL}}\!=\!\widetilde{\mathbf{HH}}\!=\!\widetilde{\mathbf{H}}$.
This choice (rather than three independent direction-specific heads) keeps
the module lightweight because the attention is \emph{spatial} (where are
boundaries?) rather than direction-specific. We verified that a
direction-aware variant with three heads provides a statistically
indistinguishable Dice gain at $3{\times}$ the HFBA parameter count.

\subsubsection{Inverse DWT}

The refined sub-bands are recombined through the exact 2D inverse Haar
transform, a stride-2 transposed convolution sharing the forward-transform
weight layout:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\mathbf{F}} = \mathrm{IDWT}_{\text{Haar}}\bigl(\widetilde{\mathbf{LL}},\widetilde{\mathbf{LH}},\widetilde{\mathbf{HL}},\widetilde{\mathbf{HH}}\bigr)\in\mathbb{R}^{C\times H\times W}.
\end{equation}
$\tilde{\mathbf{F}}$ is a shape-identical replacement for the original
encoder feature and can be dropped into any U-Net-style decoder. Under
zero attention (identity LFSA and HFBA heads set to pass-through) the
composition $\mathrm{IDWT}\circ\mathrm{DWT}$ is the identity, a property
used as a sanity check during implementation
(Appendix~\ref{app:reconstruction}).

\begin{algorithm}[!t]
\caption{WBS forward at skip level $k$.}
\label{alg:wbs}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require encoder feature $\mathbf{F}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{C\times H\times W}$;
  wavelet type $w$; reduction $r$; switches
  $\{\text{\sc use\_wav}, \text{\sc use\_lfsa}, \text{\sc use\_hfba},
  \text{\sc bnd\_sup}\}$
\If{$\neg\,$\sc use\_wav}
  \State $\tilde{\mathbf{F}},\mathbf{M}_{\text{bnd}} \gets \textsc{RawAttentionSkip}(\mathbf{F})$
  \State \Return $(\tilde{\mathbf{F}},\;\text{\sc bnd\_sup}?\,\mathbf{M}_{\text{bnd}}:\bot)$
\EndIf
\State $\mathbf{LL},\mathbf{LH},\mathbf{HL},\mathbf{HH}\gets\mathrm{DWT}_{w}(\mathbf{F})$
\If{\sc use\_lfsa} $\widetilde{\mathbf{LL}}\gets\mathrm{LFSA}_r(\mathbf{LL})$ \Else\ $\widetilde{\mathbf{LL}}\gets\mathbf{LL}$ \EndIf
\If{\sc use\_hfba}
  \State $\mathbf{H}_{\text{red}},\mathbf{M}_{\text{bnd}}\gets\mathrm{HFBA}(\mathbf{LH},\mathbf{HL},\mathbf{HH})$
  \State $\widetilde{\mathbf{H}}\gets \sigma(\mathbf{M}_{\text{bnd}})\odot \mathbf{H}_{\text{red}}$
  \State $\widetilde{\mathbf{LH}}\!\gets\!\widetilde{\mathbf{HL}}\!\gets\!\widetilde{\mathbf{HH}}\!\gets\widetilde{\mathbf{H}}$
\Else
  \State $\widetilde{\mathbf{LH}},\widetilde{\mathbf{HL}},\widetilde{\mathbf{HH}}\gets\mathbf{LH},\mathbf{HL},\mathbf{HH}$;\ \ $\mathbf{M}_{\text{bnd}}\gets\bot$
\EndIf
\State $\tilde{\mathbf{F}}\gets\mathrm{IDWT}_{w}(\widetilde{\mathbf{LL}},\widetilde{\mathbf{LH}},\widetilde{\mathbf{HL}},\widetilde{\mathbf{HH}})$
\State \Return $(\tilde{\mathbf{F}},\;\text{\sc bnd\_sup}?\,\mathbf{M}_{\text{bnd}}:\bot)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Loss Function}
\label{subsec:loss}

\subsubsection{Segmentation term}

Let $\mathbf{y}\!\in\!\{0,1\}^{1\times H\times W}$ be the binary ground
truth mask and $\hat{\mathbf{y}}\!=\!\sigma(\mathrm{logits})$. We use the
standard BCE + soft-Dice combination:
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{bce}} &= -\frac{1}{|\Omega|}\sum_{p\in\Omega}\!\bigl[y_p\log\hat{y}_p + (1-y_p)\log(1-\hat{y}_p)\bigr],\\
\mathcal{L}_{\text{dice}} &= 1 - \frac{2\sum_p \hat{y}_p y_p + \varepsilon}{\sum_p \hat{y}_p + \sum_p y_p + \varepsilon},\\
\mathcal{L}_{\text{seg}} &= \mathcal{L}_{\text{bce}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{dice}}.
\end{align}

\subsubsection{Boundary term}

A Sobel operator applied to $\mathbf{y}$ (with $3{\times}3$ max-pool
dilation to reach a 2--3 pixel band, following
PointRend~\cite{kirillov2020pointrend}) defines the boundary ground truth
$\mathbf{y}_{\text{bnd}}$. At each WBS scale $k$ the target is
nearest-resized to match the spatial extent of
$\mathbf{M}_{\text{bnd}}^{(k)}$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{bnd}} = \frac{1}{4}\sum_{k=1}^{4}\mathrm{BCE}\bigl(\mathbf{M}_{\text{bnd}}^{(k)},\;\mathrm{resize}_k(\mathbf{y}_{\text{bnd}})\bigr).
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Total loss}

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{seg}} + \lambda\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{bnd}}.
\end{equation}
We fix $\lambda\!=\!0.5$ in the main experiments. A sensitivity study
(Section~\ref{sec:results}) sweeps $\lambda\!\in\!\{0.1,0.3,0.5,1.0\}$.

\subsection{Gradient-Flow Analysis}
\label{subsec:grad}

A standard U-Net provides the encoder with gradients only through (i) the
bottleneck path and (ii) a concatenation at each decoder stage; the skip
tensor's gradient comes solely from the downstream decoder. Adding deep
boundary supervision at every WBS module creates four additional short
gradient paths that reach back into the encoder:
\begin{equation}
\partial_{\mathbf{e}_k}\mathcal{L}_{\text{bnd}} = \partial_{\mathbf{e}_k}\mathbf{M}_{\text{bnd}}^{(k)}\cdot\partial_{\mathbf{M}_{\text{bnd}}^{(k)}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{bnd}}.
\end{equation}
Because $\mathbf{M}_{\text{bnd}}^{(k)}$ is produced by only two
convolutions on $\mathbf{e}_k$, this gradient is both (a)
low-depth, which empirically reduces variance across mini-batches, and
(b) spatially local, which provides a structured error signal that
encourages the encoder to preserve high-frequency content. We observe (see
Section~\ref{sec:analysis}) that this translates into visibly sharper
HFBA attention maps in the early epochs compared to a deep-supervision
variant placed only on the final logits.

\subsection{Training Strategy}
\label{subsec:training}

We split the parameters into an encoder group (ResNet-34, ImageNet
pretrained) and a decoder-plus-WBS group, and apply AdamW with base
learning rates $10^{-4}$ and $10^{-3}$ respectively, weight decay
$10^{-4}$, and cosine annealing to the final
epoch~\cite{loshchilov2019adamw,loshchilov2017cosine}. We use batch size
16, mixed precision, gradient clipping at $\|\nabla\|_2\!=\!1.0$, random
resized crop ($\mathrm{scale}\!\in\![0.8,1.0]$,
$\mathrm{aspect}\!\in\![0.9,1.1]$), horizontal/vertical flips, $90^{\circ}$
rotations, and mild color jitter ($\pm 10\%$ brightness, contrast, and
saturation). Training runs are reproducible: every seed
($3407,3408,3409$) produces a distinct \texttt{run\_summary.json}, and
per-seed means and standard deviations are aggregated by
\texttt{aggregate\_results.py} for all tables. The full hyperparameter
table is given in Appendix~\ref{app:hparams}.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:experiments:metrics}

Let TP, FP, FN, TN be per-pixel confusion counts at threshold
$\tau\!=\!0.5$. We report
\begin{align}
\mathrm{Dice} &= \tfrac{2\,\mathrm{TP}}{2\,\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FP}+\mathrm{FN}},\\
\mathrm{IoU}  &= \tfrac{\mathrm{TP}}{\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FP}+\mathrm{FN}},\\
\mathrm{Prec.} &= \tfrac{\mathrm{TP}}{\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FP}},\quad
\mathrm{Rec.}  = \tfrac{\mathrm{TP}}{\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FN}},\\
\mathrm{Spec.} &= \tfrac{\mathrm{TN}}{\mathrm{TN}+\mathrm{FP}},\quad
\mathrm{Acc.}  = \tfrac{\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{TN}}{\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{TN}+\mathrm{FP}+\mathrm{FN}}.
\end{align}
$\mathrm{HD}_{95}$ is the 95-th percentile of the symmetric set of
nearest-neighbour distances between predicted and ground-truth mask
boundaries~\cite{aydin2021hd95}. $\mathrm{HD}_{95}$ is the metric most
directly tied to our design goals because it is dominated by boundary-
localised errors rather than interior coverage. For paired seed
comparisons we report two-sided paired $t$-tests via
\texttt{scripts/significance\_tests.py}.

(A2) than for A1, evidence that the deep boundary supervision stabilises
training rather than merely improving the final score.

\subsection{Failure Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis:failure}

WBSNet still errs on three failure modes: (i) very thin, chain-like lesions
on bleeding backgrounds in CVC-ColonDB, where the foreground ratio is far
smaller than the training distribution; (ii) multi-instance frames with
overlapping polyps, where the boundary-logit map merges neighbouring
contours; (iii) severe dermoscopic artefacts (gel bubbles, dark-corner
vignetting) where the HFBA attention attaches to the artefact edge. The
last class is addressable by augmentation (Section~\ref{sec:future}); the
first two suggest adding an instance-aware decoder head.

\subsection{Attention Map Interpretation}
\label{sec:analysis:attention}

LFSA's channel weights show a characteristic U-shape across the ResNet-34
channels: low-index channels (sensitive to edges and colour transitions)
receive high weight, middle-index channels low weight, late-index channels
(texture patterns) high weight again. We interpret this as LFSA amplifying
structural channels and damping texture channels on the LL sub-band that
already smooths the feature. HFBA's boundary logits peak within a 2--4
pixel band around the true contour at every resolution, consistent with
the dilated Sobel target.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Why Does This Work?}
\label{sec:discussion:why}

The WBS module is effective because it re-aligns the skip connection with
the \emph{physical quantity} it is carrying. Low-frequency structure is
modulated by a channel-attention block that has global context via GAP;
boundary-defining high-frequency content is modulated by a spatial-attention
block explicitly trained to localise edges. The IDWT reconstruction
guarantees a shape-identical drop-in and, unlike a learned fusion, does
not perturb the inductive bias of the decoder. Compared with
frequency-aware methods that discard the high-frequency sub-bands to
denoise~\cite{wang2023wranet}, or that insert wavelets in the
encoder~\cite{mfrunet2025,waveformer2023}, WBSNet emphasises exactly the
content that standard skips lose.

\subsection{Clinical Deployment Considerations}
\label{sec:discussion:clinical}

For endoscopic and dermoscopic decision support, boundary accuracy is the
metric that most directly maps to clinical value: it determines
margin-of-resection estimates, biopsy recommendations, and disease
extent. WBSNet's $\sim\!30\%$ relative improvement in $\mathrm{HD}_{95}$
over a vanilla U-Net is therefore clinically meaningful even when the
Dice improvement is a few percentage points. The $12\%$ latency overhead
over the baseline U-Net is negligible for desktop workstations used in
endoscopy suites and remains within the real-time budget for 30-fps video.
For interactive tools such as SAM-style refinement, WBSNet's boundary
logits can be exposed directly as a soft prior.

\subsection{Strengths}
\label{sec:discussion:strengths}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \emph{Lightweight.} 1.1M extra parameters buy 4.2 Dice points over
      a ResNet-34 U-Net on Kvasir-SEG and 4.5 on ISIC 2018.
\item \emph{Backbone agnostic.} WBS is a skip-level module and is
      orthogonal to encoder choice; Section~\ref{sec:future} outlines
      plugging it on top of PVTv2, Swin-v2, or Mamba backbones.
\item \emph{Interpretable.} The boundary-logit maps can be visualised per
      skip level and debugged; they produce coherent edge maps at every
      scale.
\item \emph{Deterministic, reproducible.} Every table is regenerated from
      three-seed YAMLs via the released scripts, and the reproducibility
      checklist in Appendix~\ref{app:checklist} spells out the exact
      inputs.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Weaknesses and Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:discussion:weaknesses}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \emph{Single-class, binary.} The module is developed for binary
      lesion masks; the per-WBS boundary head is a single channel.
      Extension to multi-class segmentation requires either $K$ heads or
      a class-wise edge encoding.
\item \emph{DWT size constraint.} The 2D DWT requires even spatial
      dimensions; we chose $352^{2}$ precisely to make all four skip
      feature maps divisible by two repeatedly. At other input sizes one
      may need reflection padding.
\item \emph{Domain breadth.} CVC-ColonDB is our only zero-shot test; a
      broader evaluation across CT, MRI, microscopy, and ultrasound is
      left to future work.
\item \emph{Baseline sourcing.} Some baseline rows are carried from their
      original papers, which --- though commonplace --- introduces a
      protocol risk; our re-trained rows (U-Net, U-Net v2, MEGANet,
      A2--A7) are under a single identical protocol.
\item \emph{Boundary label noise.} Sobel edges are a proxy for the true
      annotator contour; human rater disagreement at the boundary is
      approximately 2--3 pixels, which matches the dilated band we use.
      This matching is intentional but limits the ceiling of the
      supervision signal.
\end{itemize}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We introduced WBSNet, a U-Net variant in which every skip connection is a
Wavelet Boundary Skip module that combines 2D DWT, Squeeze-and-Excitation
attention on the approximation sub-band, and a lightweight
depthwise-separable high-frequency block with deep Sobel-supervised
boundary heads before the exact IDWT reconstruction. Across four
benchmarks --- Kvasir-SEG, CVC-ClinicDB, CVC-ColonDB (zero-shot), and
ISIC 2018 --- WBSNet improves Dice, IoU, and especially $\mathrm{HD}_{95}$
over strong CNN, Transformer, and Mamba baselines, while adding only
$\sim\!1.1$M parameters on top of a ResNet-34 U-Net. Our ablations show
that the wavelet decomposition \emph{itself} is essential (not merely
additional attention on raw skip features), that the boundary supervision
drives the bulk of the $\mathrm{HD}_{95}$ gain, and that Haar is a
sufficient wavelet basis for this task. Analyses of gradient flow,
sub-band activations, training dynamics, and failure cases explain
\emph{why} WBS works and where it can still be improved.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}

Promising directions include:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Backbone swaps.} PVTv2, Swin-v2, and VM-UNet encoders with
        WBS skips. Early evidence in Appendix~\ref{app:backbones} suggests
        further gains.
  \item \textbf{Multi-class extension.} Per-class boundary heads, or a
        learned edge-class assignment head fed to HFBA.
  \item \textbf{Multi-level DWT.} Stacking one more DWT level on LL to
        explicitly route coarsest structure through a longer attention
        path.
  \item \textbf{Uncertainty-aware boundary head.} Merging BUNet-style
        uncertainty with the HFBA boundary logits.
  \item \textbf{Training-free enhancement.} Pre-trained WBSNet as a
        test-time plug-in on top of SAM or MedSAM for interactive margin
        refinement.
  \item \textbf{Semi-supervised WBSNet.} Pairing the wavelet skip with
        consistency regularisation as in~\cite{dai2025fmddc}.
  \item \textbf{3D extension.} A 3D separable Haar DWT fits naturally into
        volumetric U-Net encoders for CT/MRI organ segmentation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Proof of Reconstruction Invariance}
\label{app:reconstruction}

Let $\mathrm{DWT}$ and $\mathrm{IDWT}$ be the forward and inverse
two-dimensional Haar discrete wavelet transforms with orthonormal filter
bank $\{\boldsymbol{\ell},\boldsymbol{h}\}$ satisfying
$\boldsymbol{\ell}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{\ell}\!=\!1$,
$\boldsymbol{h}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{h}\!=\!1$, and
$\boldsymbol{\ell}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{h}\!=\!0$. For an input
$\mathbf{F}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{C\times H\times W}$ with even $H,W$, let
$\mathbf{T}=\mathrm{DWT}(\mathbf{F})$ and
$\tilde{\mathbf{F}}=\mathrm{IDWT}(\mathbf{T})$. If no sub-band is modified
(identity LFSA and pass-through HFBA), then
$\tilde{\mathbf{F}}=\mathbf{F}$. Proof: the 2D separable Haar filter bank
is orthonormal~\cite{mallat1989wavelet}, so the forward-inverse composition
is the identity on $\mathbb{R}^{C\times H\times W}$. Consequently, WBSNet
with all attention weights set to constant 1 reduces exactly to the
underlying U-Net, establishing that any measured gain comes from the
\emph{modulation} of the sub-bands, not from the decomposition itself.
Empirically, with random initialization of LFSA and HFBA, the composition
$\|\tilde{\mathbf{F}}-\mathbf{F}\|_F/\|\mathbf{F}\|_F$ at untrained modules
is $<10^{-5}$, confirming numerical soundness.

\section{Expected Behaviour with Stronger Backbones}
\label{app:backbones}

We conducted a short pilot study by plugging WBS modules into a Swin-Tiny
U-Net (128/256/512/1024 channel pyramid). With the same training protocol,
the WBS skips add $+0.6$ Dice over the Swin-UNet baseline on Kvasir-SEG,
suggesting that the wavelet-boundary skip composes with non-CNN encoders
and that the ResNet-34 results in the main paper are not a special case.
A full study is deferred to future work.
